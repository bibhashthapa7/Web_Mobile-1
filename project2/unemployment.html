COVID-19 and its impact on the US Economy

Impact on: Unemployment

As a result of the pandemic, there was an acute and unprecedented collapse in employment which exceeded that of any recession in the last 150 years. From the end of March to the end of July, a total of 52.7 million individuals filed for unemployment insurance benefits, breaking the previous record. Although new applications for unemployment benefits have declined since pandemic-related lockdowns prompted a rise in claims at the end of March, weekly jobless claims remained close to 1.5 million at the end of the third week of July. Unemployment claims dropped out in June and the first two weeks of July, indicating that new layoffs were being countered primarily by firms hiring and returning personnel as states reopened their economies.

National employment losses peaked in April of 2020 when the country and every state had lost more jobs since February of that year than during the Great Recession. Overall, the economy has regained 76 percent of those jobs, albeit this figure masks significant variance by state. Idaho and Utah are the only states that have regained 100% of their jobs. Arizona has regained 97 percent of its lost employment, while Montana has restored 90 percent. Others have suffered worse. Wyoming has recovered just 38%, Hawaii and Alaska have regained 44%, while Louisiana has recovered 49%. However, there were 5.3 million fewer jobs in August 2021 than there were in February 2020.
